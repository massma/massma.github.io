.SHORT TERM /TONIGHT THROUGH MONDAY/...
-- Changed Discussion --
Clouds will continue to
increase across western Washington this afternoon as the next
weather system moves into the region. Highs are on track to peak
in the upper 50s along the coast where cloudy skies and rain will
limit temperatures, with areas seeing highs in the upper 60s to
low 70s further inland. An upper level trough will continue to
spread a few bands of light rain eastward throughout the evening
and into the morning Sunday, with shadowing likely to occur for
lowlands east of the Olympic Mountains and Puget Sound.

The upper level trough axis will shift inland on Saturday,
maintaining cool and unsettled conditions across the area. Showers
are favored to linger over higher terrain, with little more than
cooler conditions and cloudy skies over the lowlands after a round
of Puget Sound convergence zone showers taper off by the late
morning. Temperatures over the weekend will peak in the mid 50s
to low 60s across the lowlands.

As the trough axis shifts eastward, drier air will fill in behind
on Sunday and an upper level ridge will build into the Pacific
Northwest. This will kick off a warming and drying trend into
early next week, with temperatures rebounding into the upper 60s
and low 70s by Monday under sunny skies.

.LONG TERM /TUESDAY THROUGH FRIDAY/...Forecast models remain in
good agreement over high pressure building into western Washington
through at least Tuesday. This will maintain mostly clear and dry
conditions with a warming trend. Tuesday is on track to be the
warmest day of the period, with highs in the upper 60s along the
coast and in the upper 70s further inland. Some areas along the
Cascade Foothills and in mountain valleys may even reach the low
80s. This will bring a return to widespread Minor (Yellow)
HeatRisk across the lowlands early next week.

Wednesday brings the next chance of precipitation to western
Washington as a longwave trough centered over the Gulf of Alaska
advects a round of moisture into the region. Ensembles have kept
the best chances for precipitation over the northern and central
Cascades, with snow levels near 4500-5000 feet. Drier and mild
conditions are then favored to return on Thursday and Friday,
though ensembles show some variability in solutions.
