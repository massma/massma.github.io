Scattered showers and isolated thunderstorms continue across
western Washington as a low pressure, currently centered over the
northern tip of Vancouver Island, moves south across the area and
weakens. Showers will continue throughout the day today, finally
tapering off tonight. With snow levels right around 4000 ft,
higher elevation passes will see some light snow accumulations,
with the most snow expected across the North Cascades. Breezy
winds will also be able to subside this morning.

A transient shortwave ridge will build over the area Monday night
into Tuesday, allowing conditions to mostly dry out. Another cold
front will follow close by, bringing another shot of rain and
breezy winds on Wednesday. With lowering snow levels to around
3500 ft, mountain passes (including Stevens Pass) have better
chances of receiving significant accumulating snow. Overall the
QPF forecast is a bit lower, with around a quarter to three-
quarters of an inch through the lowlands, and up to around 2
inches through the mountains and along the Pacific coast.

Overall, the temperatures remain consistent, with highs in the
mid 50s and lows in the upper 30s to low 40s.

.LONG TERM /THURSDAY THROUGH SUNDAY/...The parent low pressure
system will move southward into the region on Thursday, keeping rain
and mountain snow showers in the forecast, though mostly in the
southern portions of the region. Looking at the upper level pattern,
the associated upper level trough will move through on Thursday,
with an upper level trough that looks to form over the East
Pacific late week and move into our area by this weekend. The mean
solution indicates that this weekend will be mostly dry, through
there are hints of a potential shortwave trough that may move
through the ridge, keeping slight chances of precipitation through
the weekend as well. Temperatures continue to remain consistent
with highs in the mid 50s and lows in the upper 30s to low 40s.

.MARINE...The pattern remains active as a low pressure system
moves southward along British Columbia today and then moves inland
by this evening. Breezy winds will gradually continue to ease
across the interior waters through the morning hours today. Winds
look to remain breezy along the outer coastal waters through much
of the day, however the bigger impact remains the seas - which
continue to range between 14-18 ft early this morning. As a
result, have extended the small craft advisories in effect
through this evening for the coastal waters (for winds and seas)
and the West Entrance of the Strait of Juan de Fuca (for seas).
Rough to very steep conditions will continue on Grays Harbor Bar
today as well.

High pressure returns to the coastal waters on Tuesday, allowing
for winds to ease and for seas to subside. The reprieve remains
short-lived, however, as a strong frontal system arrives by
Wednesday and brings another round of strong winds and significantly
building seas. Latest guidance indicates a 60-70 percent chance
of gales across the coastal waters and eastern Strait as the
frontal system moves across the region on Wednesday. The next Gulf
of Alaska low will quickly follow, dropping southward into the
region on Thursday before moving inland Thursday night into
Friday. Seas look to build back towards 16-18 ft by Thursday
morning and look to gradually subside through Friday. 14
