Area Forecast Discussion
National Weather Service Seattle WA
1011 AM PST Mon Nov 18 2024

.SYNOPSIS...Cool upper level trough will keep unsettled weather in
the area into Tuesday. Very strong surface low developing well
offshore tonight and Tuesday will be about 300 miles west of the
coast late Tuesday afternoon into Tuesday night. Even with the
low well offshore damaging winds possible Tuesday night along the
coast, portions of the Strait of Juan de Fuca and in the Cascade
foothills. The low will weaken and track north Wednesday. Front
spinning out of the low moving through Wednesday. Weaker lows will
develop off the coast for the remainder of the week keeping wet
and breezy at times conditions over the area into the weekend.

.SHORT TERM /TODAY THROUGH WEDNESDAY/...
-- Changed Discussion --
The latest forecast
remains on track with no updates this morning. Little change in
the pattern through tonight with the cool upper level trough
remaining overhead. Showers continuing through the overnight hours
with a chance of thunderstorms along the coast, Lower Chehalis
Valley and Southwest Interior. Snow levels hovering around 2000
feet today lowering a little tonight. Weakening orographics in the
afternoon will result in less snow accumulation for the Cascades.
Snow showers continuing into early Tuesday morning but with
lesser accumulations expected will let the Winter Storm Warning
expire at 10 am this morning. Highs today in the mid 40s. Lows
tonight mostly in the mid to upper 30s. With a little break in the
cloud cover some locations in the Southwest Interior will be near
freezing. Could see some rain snow mixed showers in the early
morning hours on some of the higher hills.

Shower activity dries up Tuesday morning. The main weather event
in this forecast package will occur Tuesday night. Very strong
surface low developing well offshore tonight into Tuesday. The
low is currently way back near 165W. Very cold air spilling out of
the Gulf of Alaska will run into warm sub tropical air around
150W tonight. Throw in a 160 knot jet core and you get
bombogenesis (a low that deepens 24 millibars or more in 24 hours).
The operational ECMWF has the surface low going from 1000 mb at
06z Tuesday near 41N/144W to 970 mb at 18z Tuesday near 44N/132W
to 950 mb at 00z Wednesday near 47N/131W. The operational GFS is a
little stronger with the low, surface pressure bottoming out at
940 mb, which gives the low a little further westward track.
Either way the low is close enough to cause damaging winds over
portions of Western Washington Tuesday night. The strongest winds
are expected along the coast, western portion of the Strait of
Juan de Fuca and the Cascade foothills. Strong easterly winds
could get further down the I-90 corridor into the Bellevue and
Lake Sammamish. Easterly gradients increasing rapidly late Tuesday
afternoon into Tuesday night. Gradients through the Strait of
Juan de Fuca, Quillayute to Bellingham, peaking around -10 mb
while the cross Cascade gradients drop down to -15 mb. Not only do
the cross Cascade gradients decrease rapidly winds aloft continue
to show good flow reversal peaking at 06z Wednesday. Model 850 mb
winds easterly at 30-40 knots while the winds at 700 mb are south
southwesterly at 40-60 knots. This flow reversal brings up the
possibility of mountain wave activity in the foothills Tuesday
night in addition to the strong easterly winds caused by the
gradients. Always hard to predict where the mountain waves will
surface if they develop. In past mountain wave events Enumclaw,
Buckley, Black Diamond and as far west as Maple Valley have been
hit by surfacing mountain waves. Will issue a high wind watch for
Tuesday night for the coast, Western Strait of Juan de Fuca, East
Puget Sound Lowlands and the Bellevue area. It will be windy
outside of these areas Tuesday night as well but just not as
strong as in the watch areas.

Front spinning out of the low spreading rain over the area
during the day Tuesday with the rain continuing Tuesday night.
Easterly gradients will help keep snow levels near pass level
Tuesday night for a few inches of new snow. Forecasting around
0.50 inches of water for Stevens and Snoqualmie pass Tuesday
night.

Low moving off to the north and weakening Wednesday. Front moving
through during the day with rain changing to showers. Winds easing
during the day. Highs warming up to near 50. Snow levels remaining
below the passes with a few more inches of new snow for the
Cascades.

.LONG TERM /THURSDAY THROUGH SUNDAY/...Extended models in good
agreement Thursday with large upper level low off the coast and
another impulse spinning out of the low moving through Western
Washington for more rain and mountain snow. Snow levels slowly
rising to the 3000 to 4000 foot range. After Thursday models
solutions are inconsistent. Another impulse spinning out of the
low Friday. The ECMWF brings this feature over Western Washington
while the GFS takes it inland mostly to the south. The GFS
ensembles are more in line with the ECMWF so will go rain at times
again Friday. The GFS continues with the idea of most of the
energy moving inland south of the area over the weekend while the
ECMWF is further north. The operational ECMWF is drier Sunday but
the ECMWF ensembles do not agree with the operational run being an
outlier. End result of all this will keep the rain likely/rain at
times forecast going into the weekend. Snow levels will remain
near at least Stevens Pass levels except down around Mount Rainier
adding to the November snowpack.

Felton

.AVIATION...Zonal flow aloft as broad troughing briefly moves
through the region, with a weak shortwave ridge building through
western Washington this afternoon. Mostly MVFR conditions out
there early this morning as lingering showers continue to move
through the region, providing an excellent saturated layer for
MVFR to stick through the majority of the morning hours. Showers
will gradually taper through the day as brief ridging takes
place, with a rebound to VFR possible into the afternoon.
Surface winds generally S/SW 8 to 12 knots this morning, with
some places still seeing gusts up to 15 to 20 knots. Winds will
slightly decrease this afternoon, but still keeping up around 5
to 10 knots. Low ceilings and clouds look to return overnight.

KSEA...MVFR early this morning as residual showers remain in the
area. Some improvement is likely later this morning into the
afternoon, with a return to VFR around 15z-18z as ceilings
gradually lift. VFR is expected to stick around through the
evening, before ceilings start to fall back down to MVFR overnight
into Tuesday morning. S/SW winds this morning 10 to 15 knots with
some occasional gusts up to 20 kts. Winds will slightly lower but
maintain 8 to 12 knots into the evening.

.MARINE...Recent buoy observations continue to show heightened
seas over the coastal waters, around 14 to 18 feet. A small craft
advisory remains over the coastal waters and has been extended
into Tuesday for heightened seas, as winds will generally be
benign.

A robust sub 960 millibar surface low will deepen over the
offshore waters Tuesday into Tuesday night, bringing significant
seas and winds over our coastal waters. Latest guidance shows
higher probabilities of Storm Force Winds over the coastal waters,
therefore, the Storm Force Watch has been upgraded to a Storm
Force Warning. Pressure gradients will also tighten over the
Strait of Juan De Fuca, with a strong south/southeast wind and
some models indicating higher gusts up to 50 kt possible. With
that, also have issued a Storm Force Watch through the Strait of
Juan De Fuca. Elsewhere, most of the interior waters show signs of
high end small craft advisory strength winds, or even gale force
(especially in the Northern Interior Waters, and possibly even the
Puget Sound). Additional headlines for these areas will be
needed.

Combined seas this morning around 14 to 18 feet, and will briefly
lower to 10 to 13 feet by late tonight. Seas will then rapidly
build upwards to around 23 to 27 feet over the coastal waters by
Tuesday evening. The highest waves (23 to 27 feet) will be mainly
in the outer water zones. In the inner water zones, can expect
seas generally around 17 to 22 feet.

Mazurkiewicz

.HYDROLOGY...
-- Changed Discussion --
Precipitation over the Olympics Tuesday night into
Wednesday could push the already running high Skokomish above flood
stage Wednesday. Snow levels down to around 2000 ft during the
heaviest precipitation could limit the runoff and any flooding
chance. The flood watch for Mason county will continue. Rises also
forecast for the remainder of the rivers this week but the
combination of relatively low snow levels and the lack of heavy
mountain precipitation will keep rivers in their banks.

Felton/Bower
