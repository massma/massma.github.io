.SHORT TERM /TONIGHT THROUGH SUNDAY/...
-- Changed Discussion --
Just a few isolated
showers remain out there this afternoon across western Washington.
Additional weak waves of precipitation will spread across the
region tonight through Thursday bringing light rain and mountain
snow. If enough cooling can occur between waves there could be a
slight chance few wet snowflakes mixed in with the rain in lowland
areas from roughly south Everett and Fall City northward.
However, no accumulation is expected.

Easterly winds are expected to gradually increase Thursday
afternoon through Friday morning before decreasing late Friday
morning. Favored areas such as the western slopes/foothills of the
Cascades as well as the Pacific Coast will see the highest wind
speeds. The most likely peak gusts are expected to be around 35
mph with a 15% chance of gusts to around 45 mph.

More widespread rain and mountain snow is expected Friday and
Saturday, with an additional wave on Sunday. This will be the
period with the highest precipitation totals. Still QPF amounts
will be such that only the Skokomish River in Mason County is at
risk of reaching flood stage. In the mountains, snow levels will
generally hover between 3000-4000 feet. While Snoqualmie Pass may
struggle to pick up significant snowfall at road level, the higher
mountains and passes will pick up several inches each day. This
time frame will be very borderline for Winter Weather Advisory
criteria and we will continue to monitor the forecast.

.LONG TERM /MONDAY THROUGH WEDNESDAY/...Longwave ridging attempts
to set up along the west coast next week which should result in
somewhat drier and warmer conditions. However, the details here
continue to vary between ensemble members. In general, Monday and
Tuesday favor the driest conditions. This could then be followed
by a period of dirty ridging with the region seeing occasional
glancing blows of systems moving over the top of the ridge.

The Climate Prediction Center 6-10 and 8-14 day outlooks indicate
the potential for this trend to continue into the week-2 time,
highlighting above-normal temperatures as well as near-normal
precipitation across far northern WA and below-normal
precipitation elsewhere.
-Wolcott-

.MARINE...
-- Changed Discussion --
An occluded surface low remains over the NE PAC with
higher pressure over the interior. This gradient will maintain SCA
over the coastal waters (mostly outer) and neighboring West Strait
due to winds late tonight into Thursday morning. As winds decrease
by Thursday afternoon, seas are to top out near 10 ft, maintaining
SCA conditions through at least Friday. On top of that, an
additional disturbance is forecast to track over the coastal waters
with gusty offshore winds with additional headlines likely through
the strait for Friday as well.

Seas 10 to 12 ft this evening before easing somewhat Thursday
morning. That said, the risk for seas exceeding 10 ft remains in
place into Thursday but mainly for the outer waters. Seas ramp up
again late Thursday night before peaking Friday afternoon at 10 to
13 ft before easing over the weekend.

McMillian
-- End Changed Discussion --

.HYDROLOGY...
-- Changed Discussion --
Several weather systems will move across Western
Washington through the weekend resulting in additional
precipitation. Each of these systems will be fairly progressive
however, which will limit the total rainfall accumulation with
each system. At this time, the heaviest rainfall amounts are
expected over the southern Olympics, with amounts ranging 2 to 4
inches Friday through Sunday. This will result in rises on the
Skokomish River, which will approach flood stage over the weekend.
Although rises on rivers are expected elsewhere, no additional
river flooding is forecast.
