.SYNOPSIS...
-- Changed Discussion --
An upper level ridge centered offshore will gradually
build into the region through the early portion of the week. This
will lead to dry conditions and chilly overnight temperatures.
The ridge will weaken by midweek and a series of systems will
bring a return of lowland rain and mountain snow Friday into the
weekend, with a threat of lowland moving into next week.

.SHORT TERM /TODAY THROUGH WEDNESDAY/...
-- Changed Discussion --
High pressure remains
dominant across the Pacific NW with dry and stable weather
continuing. Expect chilly morning temperatures in the 20s and 30s
each morning with highs mainly in the 40s. Offshore winds will
keep skies clear with plenty of sunshine. 33

.LONG TERM /THURSDAY THROUGH SUNDAY/...The ridge starts to
weaken/flatten by Thursday as a trough moves in from the west.
This system will spread light warm frontal precip to the region
Thursday night with the main cold frontal band moving inland early
Friday. A flip to onshore flow will keep lowland temps above
freezing with rain expected. Up in the mountains, snow levels
hover around 3,000-4,000 ft with a few inches of snow in the
Cascades and passes. Mountain snow may be heavy at times Friday
night as the trough axis passes.

A cool upper level trough keeps snow levels low on Saturday and
there`s a rain/snow mix possible in the lowlands (although NBM
probabilities of an inch or more are generally low). Lowland snow
threats continue into early next week as the cool upper low stalls
and spins offshore. Minor to moderate winter storm impacts are
possible depending on how much moisture streams in. 33
-- End Changed Discussion --

.AVIATION...
-- Changed Discussion --
An upper level ridge will remain centered offshore
resulting in northerly flow aloft today. As the center of high
pressure drifts southward tonight, flow aloft will shift to become
more northwesterly for Tuesday. At the surface, offshore flow will
continue with gradients relaxing through the day and into Tuesday,
resulting in widespread wind speeds of 5 kts or less.

Widespread VFR conditions in place and look to remain that way for
the majority of the TAF period, although seeing an isolated ob of
IFR conditions out by Port Townsend. Inherited forecasts suggest
that OLM and PWT could see vis restrictions emerge after 12Z, and
guidance still points in that direction. Given how prone these sites
are to such development, persistence forecasting seems reasonable at
this time. These two sites could see fog re-emerge late
tonight/early Tuesday morning. For remaining sites, VFR conditions
to continue, although some models are hinting at more widespread fog
prospects. This solution seems to be in the minority at this time
however, and as such, aside from the two terminals already listed,
will likely keep VFR in place.

KSEA...VFR conditions to persist throughout the TAF period. While
some finer scale models do hint at fog or at least some vis
restriction developing after 09Z overnight, I recall seeing similar
solutions 24 hours ago which have, at the time of this writing, not
materialized. As such, will stick with the majority of solutions at
this time, but will brief on the prospect so at least the
possibility is in mind. Winds mostly north to northeasterly with
speeds under 5 kts this morning with a shift to more northerly this
afternoon. This might give speeds a slight boost, but even then do
not expect speeds to exceed the 4-8 kts range.
